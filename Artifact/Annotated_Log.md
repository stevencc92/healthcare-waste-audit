# Exhibit A: Food Usage Log (Annotated)

This document highlights structural issues in the food usage log that prevent meaningful measurement, comparison, or accountability.

---

## 1. Undefined Unit of Measurement

The log records quantities “in servings,” but provides no definition of what a serving represents.

As a result:
- Units are inconsistent across menu items
- Quantities cannot be compared or aggregated
- Recorded values lack physical meaning (e.g., weight or volume)

Without a defined unit, numerical entries cannot support analysis.

---

## 2. Inconsistent Application Across Items

The same unit (“servings”) is applied to fundamentally different foods (e.g., pancakes, tofu scramble, potatoes), each of which may vary significantly in size, preparation method, and yield.

This prevents:
- Cross-item comparison
- Standardized waste tracking
- Trend analysis over time

---

## 3. Presence of a Census Without Integration

The log includes a census count indicating the number of individuals served.

However, census values are not tied to:
- Planned production
- Expected yield
- Per-person consumption metrics

As a result, demand signals exist but are not operationalized.

---

## 4. “Used” Column Is Not Utilized

The form includes a column labeled “Used,” but entries are consistently left blank or recorded as zero.

This indicates that:
- Usage is not being calculated
- The accounting identity (starting + additions − remaining = used) is not enforced
- The form captures activity, not consumption

---

## 5. Lack of Temporal or Comparative Context

Entries are recorded per meal without:
- Reference to prior meals
- Baselines or targets
- Historical comparison

This prevents:
- Detection of improvement or regression
- Accountability over time
- Evaluation of interventions

---

## Summary Observation

The log appears structured to demonstrate documentation rather than to produce actionable insight.

While it records numbers, those numbers cannot be reliably interpreted, compared, or used to inform decisions.
