# Measurement Requirements for Actionable Food Waste Metrics

## Purpose

This section defines the minimum requirements a food usage metric must meet in order to support meaningful analysis, accountability, and decision-making.

These requirements are independent of any specific organization or implementation and represent baseline conditions for valid measurement.

---

## 1. Defined Units of Measurement

A metric must use clearly defined, consistent units.

For food usage and waste tracking, this typically means:
- Weight (e.g., pounds, kilograms), or
- Standardized count (when portion sizes are fixed and enforced)

Without a defined unit:
- Recorded values lack physical meaning
- Comparisons across items or time periods are invalid
- Aggregation produces misleading results

A numerical value without a unit is not a metric.

---

## 2. Consistent Application Across Items

Measurement units must be applied consistently across all recorded items.

Using the same unit label for items with vastly different sizes, yields, or preparation methods introduces ambiguity and prevents meaningful comparison.

Consistency is required for:
- Cross-item analysis
- Trend detection
- Identification of high-waste categories

---

## 3. Demand Context (Denominators)

Usage metrics must be interpretable relative to demand.

This requires tying food quantities to at least one denominator, such as:
- Number of individuals served
- Planned production volume
- Expected yield

Without a denominator:
- Efficiency cannot be assessed
- Overproduction cannot be identified
- Waste rates cannot be calculated

Demand context transforms raw counts into insight.

---

## 4. Closure and Accounting Identity

A valid usage metric must satisfy a basic accounting relationship:

> **Starting Quantity + Additions − Remaining = Used**

If usage is not calculated or enforced:
- Consumption cannot be verified
- Errors cannot be detected
- Accountability cannot be established

The presence of a “Used” field without enforcement undermines the integrity of the entire system.

---

## 5. Temporal Comparability

Metrics must support comparison over time.

This requires:
- Stable definitions
- Consistent units
- Repeated measurement under similar conditions

Without temporal comparability:
- Trends cannot be identified
- Improvements cannot be verified
- Interventions cannot be evaluated

Tracking without comparison is documentation, not analysis.

---

## Summary

For a food usage log to support accountability, it must produce data that is:
- Defined
- Comparable
- Interpretable
- Verifiable

If any of these conditions are missing, the system cannot reliably inform decisions, regardless of how frequently data is recorded.
